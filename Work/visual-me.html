Stretching me out

A visual design experiment of what my behaviors would be at the extremes

Started in May 2015

The Context

Over the past few weeks, I've been spending a lot of time with myself. I moved into a new apartment and I'm living alone for the first time, I had a leg injury and had to take a break from activities, and work got a little busier. I began thinking about the person I've become. At 23 years old, what is my character? How do I behave in certain situations? What is it that makes me unique, interesting? There had to be parts of me that light up, that always came to the rescue, that I could count on.

In an attempt to highlight a few of those behaviors, I decided to visualize the hyperbole of each one. An exagerration that was borderline insane. This allows me to play around a little with the creative expression.

From a development perspective, I hope that outlining these habits or behaviors makes me more self-aware of how I am, how I can use these them to help others or help myself, and how I can tone them down if appropriate.

The medium I'm using for these visuals is Inkscape, a free alternative to Adobe Illustrator and a software I've wanted to learn. This is my first project in Inkscape. Some illustrations are up, more are to come.

For the past year, I've developed the terribly expensive habit of stopping at Starbucks on the way to work everyday. If I didn't have time in the morning, I'd make an afternoon run instead. If I didn't empty a Starbucks cup that day, some part of me was missing. More recently, I've toned the habit down a bit. But mind you, I was collecting a lot of stars for my dedication...
Maybe my tendency to ask questions comes from the work I do as a user experience designer. After all, I try to understand the way others live. But back in college, I think I consciously made this behavior change. I came to view my conversations with others as a knowledge exchange. A way to learn something new and hopefully reciprocate. For a few years now, I have been happy to let the person talk in order to learn from them. Hopefully it's making me a better listener. This illustration is an ode to a favorite college game, Kings, where picking the Queen card meant you became the question master - tricking others into answering your questions.
I fell in love with international politics in high school. In Model UN, I represented some rebellious countries - Israel, Iran, to name two. The more volatile the country, the more research I needed to be an effective diplomat. Over time, that developed into an interest for politics in the places I was living - India, the US, a little while in Europe. Now pile on TV shows like Homeland, and it's a career daydream waiting to happen. Have to say, I certainly have the BBC intel to get me going in the right direction.
